The Women who Served: Women’s Battalions in Revolutionary Russia

The first world war had a massive impact on the Russian Empire. As millions of men were sent to the front, women took on domestic jobs, as they did in the west. By 1916, 35% of railroad workers were women, as were many factory workers. In fact, a small number of women served in the Russian military, with specific permission from the czar. However, in March of 1917, the Czar was overthrown in favor of the provisional government.  This led to massive social change, especially for women, and it was this unique social situation that led to the creation of women’s combat units, spearheaded by decorated veteran Maria Leontevna Botchkareva. Though problematic in certain ways, and met with some resistance, this marked a remarkable and revolutionary step forward in women’s right, and yet, due to the cataclysm that was the October revolution and the Russian civil war, these women were all but forgotten.

Botchkareva was born a Siberian peasant in 1889. She worked from the age of 8 and married quite young. She fled the abuse of her first husband, only for her second husband to become abusive as well. When war broke out in 1914, she felt that, “My country called me. An irresistible force from within pulled me.” She petitioned the czar and was permitted to join the army. Incredible though this may seem, she was not unique in this, with a handful of other women serving during this time, all with specific permission from the czar. Along with these other women, she was accepted into the army, and after an initial period of shock, they were accepted by their respective units, foreshadowing the social acceptance of the women’s battalions (Stockdale). Botchkareva was not merely accepted by her battalion, in fact, in 1915, she carried approximately 50 wounded men back from no man’s land and was recommended for an Order of the 4th Degree medal, “for distinguished valor.” In this same battle, her leg was injured, but she returned to the front, only to be shot in the hand, and yet earned a recommendation for the Cross of St George Forth Degree, though she did not receive it because of her gender. By early 1916, she earned a Gold Medal of the 2nd Degree, and was promoted to a corporal, leading a platoon of 11 men, until she was wounded and paralyzed in June of 1916. In spite of literal paralysis, she learned how to walk again, and when she returned to the front at the end of the year, she was placed in charge of 70 men. (Sowers)

Meanwhile, the Russian Empire was collapsing. The first world war took a drastic toll on the Russian empire. Russia lost between 1.7 and 2.2 million men, more than any other nation except perhaps Germany. While the empire was massive, it simply lacked the leadership and the industrial capacity to fight this war, and as supplies grew short, workers on the home front began to riot, while discipline began to break down at the front. On March 8th, 1917, women factory workers led the bread riot that began the February revolution, and by March 16th, the Provisional Government was established (Демонстрация)(“Russian”). While this government and revolution are often overlooked today, primarily because it was followed by the much more consequential October revolution and the accent of the Bolsheviks, it was by no means inconsequential, especially in the world of feminism. The provisional government implemented universal suffrage in July, and women were allowed to serve as attorneys and on juries, and were guaranteed equal opportunities and pay in civil service. It was this revolutionary atmosphere that gave Botchkareva the opportunity to establish a women’s battalion, however, it was the desperation of high command that propelled it. After the revolution, soldier’s committees were established in the army, and though on paper they would give soldiers a democratic voice in the war, in practice it allowed the demoralized soldiers to vote on whether or not to execute their orders. Furthermore, killing commanding officers became relatively common. These soldiers were not alone in their disgust for the war, rather, it was exceptionally unpopular throughout the country. Faced with this challenge, high command decided that a decisive victory was needed to rally the nation, and inspire the people, much like the wars of the French revolution. This was to be the Kerensky Offensive. High command began to create so-called “shock battalions” which were composed of revolutionary civilians and would lead the rest of the army into battle and inspire them (Stockdale).

On leave in May of 1917, and disgusted by the decaying discipline amongst soldiers, Botchkareva proposed the creation of an all-female shock battalion. Her cause was taken up by the President of the Duma (parliament), and by high command (Stockdale). Speaking at the Mariynski Theatre, she called out to “women whose hearts are loyal, whose souls are pure, whose aims are high. With such women setting an example of self-sacrifice, you men will realize your duty in this grave hour!” Despite promising these women “punishment for even slight acts of disobedience,” despite prohibiting a soldier’s committee, and in the face of the horrors of the war, nearly 2000 women signed the pledge (Botchkareva). According to Botchkareva, the signatures came both from the most illustrious families in the country, and from poor farmworkers and servants. Because the battalion was to see service in the Kerensky Offensive mere weeks later, the number of soldiers was whittled down to just 300, but this did nothing to reduce enthusiasm, and no fewer than 11 more combat units were officially created, along with several non-combat units, a naval unit, and numerous unofficial units. The treatment these women received was remarkable for the time. They were on equal footing with their male counterparts, with the same wages, the same rights, and the intent to give the same pensions, thought due to the October Revolution, this did not occur (Stockdale).

However, the situation was not perfect. These women joined the battalion at the expense of their femininity, with the express intent that they should no longer be women, but instead just soldiers. They were required to keep their hair cut short at all times (Женские), and to sleep on boards, so as to toughen them. (Sowers). They were explicitly ordered not to fraternize with the male soldiers, out of fear of “little soldiers”, and out of fear they would interrupt their discipline (Stockdale). These women believed they had to set aside all aspects of their femininity to become good soldiers (Sowers). Furthermore, these battalions were created with the explicit aim to shame men into service, and not out of some feeling of equality. This removal of the women from their femininity, and association of their femininity with shame for the male soldiers reveals the serious issues with the women’s battalions, but does not devalue their accomplishments, nor undermine their radical nature.

Despite the radical nature of the situation, the women were broadly accepted, with General Sir Alfred Knox, British military liaison to the Russian army, “They made the best show of any soldiers I have seen since the Revolution” (Stockdale). By be the end of the summer, the sight of a short-haired armed woman in uniform was normalized in St. Petersburg (Женский). While there was some resistance from the army, with one general writing that he saw the women as “an offence to the front,” they were broadly accepted by the troops on the ground, and most resistance was against the very concept of shock battalions, not the women’s battalions specifically. (Stockdale) In fact, after the Kerensky Offensive, as a de-facto truce was established between the demoralized Russian army and the German army, the women’s battalion was attacked by the men several times, including one incident involving a machine gun, but not because they were women, but rather because Botchkareva refused to give up the war, and refused to give up the discipline in her unit (Sowers).

Though none of the other women’s battalions saw active duty service, the Petrograd Women's Battalion was called upon to defend the Winter Palace from the Bolsheviks during the October revolution, literally at the same time as Botchkareva’s battalion fought male soldiers over her ongoing commitment to the war. The Russian civil war between the Bolshevik red army and the anti-Bolshevik white army broke out, and after refusing to join Lenin, Botchkareva fled to the west, primarily to get international aid against the red army in the civil war. She was executed in 1920 by the red army after her capture at the age of 31 (Sowers). Though she was recognized by feminists of her day, her accomplishments, and the story of these women had no major impact on feminism globally and remain largely forgotten to this day (Stockdale).

Much of the difficulty in understanding the story of these women, and why they had so little impact on history, lies in the complex political situation of Russia. While, as discussed earlier, there was opposition and violence against these women, it is impossible to determine how much of the violence was political in nature, and how much was due to the gender of these soldiers. The affiliation of these women with the provisional government made them targets of the rising power of Bolsheviks in the army. This did not just make them bigger targets at the time, indeed, they were ignored by the Soviets because the Soviet government ignored the war, and demonized the provisional government, and because of their role fighting against the revolution at the palace, because of Botchkareva’s rejection of Lenin and Trotsky, and more broadly because many of these women fought for the white army, the stories of these women were not often told in the USSR. When they when discussed, they were portrayed as ignorant, bourgeoise women, who did not know what they were doing, with one soviet source describing the women of the Petrograd battalion as being happy that they were disbanded (Астрахан). The Soviet government found it inconvenient to portray the provisional government as a progressive one, and as one inspiring patriotism, and so they didn’t, and as the stories of these women would portray them as such, their stories were not told. It is therefore difficult to say that these women, who were sometimes accepted and sometimes targeted, were targeted because they were women, and yet it is equally difficult to eliminate the possibility altogether. Though they had little historical impact, it cannot be said that this was not historic, and therefore the earth-shaking convulsions of their nation must bear at least some of that blame. Though their story is complex, and not one of pure or ideal progress, and though it is all but forgotten now, these women were undoubtedly trailblazers.
