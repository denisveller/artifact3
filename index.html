The Pre-War Years
The Russian Empire under Tsarist rule had progressed much slower than its Western neighbors in industrialization. By the time Russia began industrializing in the late 19th century, the country was plagued by social, political, and economic unrest. An embarrassing failure in the Russo-Japanese War (1904-1905), a rapid increase in population due to industrialization, and an ensuing food shortages, led to the Russian Revolution of 1905. Revolutionaries consisted of Socialists, Marxists, and Center-Liberals who recognized the need for change in the government. While the Tsar was not overthrown, he was forced to compromise in the form of the October Manifesto, which created an elected national legislature named the Duma. No legislation could be passed without the approval of the Duma, therefore limiting the Tsar’s autocratic powers. However, the Duma's authority was quickly undermined by the Tsar, and this would prove only a band-aid to a glaringly large wound, due to the events of the following decade.
The First World War
Russia entered the World War in defense of Serbia against Austria-Hungary and Germany in 1914. While not obligated to do so, Russian leaders had a political motivation, as a failure to aid Serbia would make Russia appear weak, thereby losing any chance of controlling the Balkans. Russia was easily outmatched in terms of firepower compared to its enemies: Germany had industrialized far faster and earlier than Russia, and its armies were much better equipped. Despite this, Russian forces scored several victories in key battles and were successful in diverting German manpower to the Eastern Front. Eventually casualties proved to be too high, and the war effort became increasingly demoralizing. By 1915, many Russian soldiers were deployed to the battlefield without basic equipment like a firearm or boots due to equipment shortages, and were told to simply pick up weapons they found. Russia had the largest casualties among any of the combatants, suffering roughly 3.3 million deaths. The war had stirred even more food shortages, the government appeared more corrupt than ever due to influencers like Rasputin, and the Duma, the powerless Russian Parliment, was repeatedly dissolved by the Tsar. This directly led to the Revolutions of 1917
The February Revolution
The February Revolution began on March 8th, 1917. Industrial workers, many of them women, were on strike in the streets. Peasants were clamoring for bread. Theyformed large crowds and began to protest around the area of the capital. The workers went into the streets, chanting: "Down with Autocracy!"" and "Down with War!" On March 11th, the Tsar ordered the Petrograd police to fire upon the protestors, resulting in hundreds of deaths. The scale of the demonstrations and a lack of faith in the Tsar caused the police to waiver, and by March 12th, the Duma formed a new provisional government. On March 15th, Tsar Nicholas formally abdicated the throne. The autocratic, conservative monarchy was overthrown and succeeded by a liberal, progressive Provisional Government that seemed to support democratization.
The October Revolution
While the February Revolution had succeeded in overthrowing centuries of autocratic rule in Russia, the Provisional Government that formed was composed primarily of the wealthy, land-owning bourgeois. The government continued the war effort on the Eastern Front, which only worsened food shortages. Bolsheviks, who represented much of the poor working class, were frustrated by the liberal ideologies and ineffectiveness of the Provisional Government and desired Communism, not Liberalism. The Bolsheviks established the Petrograd Soviet, or Petrograd Worker's Union, to rival the Provisional Government, and on November 6th, the Bolshevik Party, led by Vladimir Lenin, executed a coup on the Provisional Government, and had seized most government offices after just two days. By November 8th, Vladimir Lenin had become the leader of Communist Russia, but his power was by no means solidified, as Russia was plunged into the Russian Civil War.
The Russian Civil War
Though the October Revolution was a success for the Bolsheviks, their transition to power was not met without resistance. Right-wing and some moderate factions formed the White Army, while socialist-supporting factions formed the Red Army. The five year conflict was arguably a continuation of World War I, as the Bolshevik government signed a treaty with the German forces to escape the rising economic and death toll. Russia’s withdrawal meant the collapse of the Eastern Front, and consequently the Allied Powers were politically motivated to support the White Army in hopes that they would prevail and reenter the war. The The Red Army’s effective victory by 1921 solidified the Bolsheviks as the sole political party within Russia, at the cost of roughly 7,000,000 casualties, mostly civilians.
Lenin's Rule
The Russian Civil War (1917-1923) ended with the Red Army (Bolsheviks) establishing the Soviet Union, and the White Army (capitalists, monarchists, social democrats) collapsed. Vladimir Lenin was solidified as the Chairman of the Soviet Union and the Bolshevik-dominated government began its sweeping reforms. After the short-lived War Communism policy (forced requisition led to many peasant revolts and therefore a food shortage), Lenin’s government shifted to the New Economic Policy (NEP), a reform that provided room for entrepreneurs to establish private businesses in smaller industries. The majority of key industries remained under state control, and monetary reform in 1923 ended the despised forced requisitions. While the NEP was a successful short term solution, the end-goal of a fully socialist economy remained to be achieved. Lenin’s death in 1924 moved the goal further to the future as the question of succession was rampant for several years, until Stalin took power.
Stalin's Rule
Stalin’s reign from 1928 to 1953 included a rapid increase in industrialization and collectivization, but also several Purges and intentional starvation in the country of Ukraine, with death tolls in the estimated millions. It was under Stalin’s reign that the gulag system became most prominent. Under this system, roughly eighteen million people were sent to forced labor camps in Siberia. Anyone could go to the gulags, from petty criminals to political dissenters. Stalin was an extreme Russian nationalist, propagandist, and advocated for arguably the most censorship out of any leader in the history of the USSR. Under his leadership, the Soviet Union also became one of the world’s two superpowers, and anti-Communist policy from Western powers in the Cold War contributed to his tightening of control over the USSR.
The Second World War
The Soviet Union’s involvement in the Second World War started in 1939, when it signed a pact with the Nazi Germany to divide Poland and the rest of Eastern Europe. However, after collaborating with Germany for around 2 years, a 3,000,000 man German force invaded the USSR in 1941. This was the largest invasion in history, and Nazi forces overwhelmed Soviet troops in the initial weeks. Despite this, as the operation continued, the Red Army began clawing back via continued domestic support and a scorched earth policy that hindered the invaders. By 1945 the Soviets had successfully pushed the Nazis back to Germany. The Red Army was immensly important to Allied vicoty, as Nazi Germany had routed a significant amount of resources to the Eastern Front. The Soviets suffered by far the largest number of casualties of any nation, an estimated 25,000,000. The aftermath of the war led the Soviet Union to become one of two global superpowers.
Khrushcev's Rule
The Khrushchev period (1953 to 1964) saw a reversal from the Stalin era, as Khrushchev publicly denounced aspects of Stalin’s leadership and laxed control on media censorship. Khrushchev also reversed the USSR’s foreign policy by easing hostilities with Western powers. His tenure also saw the great success of the Russian space program, and even signed a Nuclear Test-Ban Treaty with Western powers. He restored the citizenship of falsely accused deported nationalities, of which there were many due to Stalin’s extreme Russian nationalism. Khrushchev’s era saw a genuine concern for the many minorities in the USSR, a desire for peace with the West, and the restoration of freedom to Soviet writers, but was an economic failure in terms of industrialization reform, a declining agricultural policy, and the acceleration of the costly Arms Race.

ANNA AKHMATOVA | АННА АХМАТОВА


Anna Akhmatova is one of the greatest Russian poets of the 20th century, but in the West, hardly a soul knows her name. This is quite tragic, as in the eyes of many scholars, she is seen as a social prophet. As one of the firsthand witnesses to the rise of the Soviet Union and the subsequent tyrannical rule of Joseph Stalin, Akhmatova was able to provide incredible insight on the subjects of suffering and oppression. In many ways, she was the silent, anguished voice of the entire Russian people during Stalin’s reign of terror.

Anna Akhmatova was born Anna Andreevna Gorenko on June 11, 1889 at Bolshoy Fontan, a small town on the Black Sea, though when she was very young her family moved to a small town called Tsarkoye Selo, outside of St. Petersburg. Her family was upper-class, descending from both Russian and Ukrainian royalty. She began to write poetry when she was eleven years old, where her main inspiration was one of Russia’s other great poets: Aleksandr Pushkin.

In 1903 she met her first husband, Nikolay Gumilev, with whom she would have her first and only child in 1912. Later on in her life, her son would be forced in and out of prison by the Soviet regime, and this had a great influence on much of Akhmatova’s work.

In 1910, Akhmatova teamed with famed Russian poets Osip Mandelstam and Sergey Gorodetsky to form the Guild of Poets, going on to create the Acmeist school of poetry, a direct response to the symbolist school that had dominated Russian Poetry for decades. It was in these years between 1910 and the start of the First World War that Anna Akhmatova became well known to the Russian people with the publishing of her first collection of poetry known as Vecher (Evening in English).

Akhmatova lived a very Bohemian lifestyle prior to the First World War. Due to the absence of her husband, who constantly partook in expeditions to Africa, she had many affairs. Still, she was well respected and loved by the Russian intelligentsia for her good looks and charming nature. Her second book, Chetki (Rosary in English) was the most popular of her books during this time. The poems in both Vecher and Chetki both tackle the intensely personal and intricate aspects of love.

When the First World War broke out, there was a distinct shift in the poetry of Anna Akhmatova. Gone were the poems which spoke of unrequited love and penitence for a bohemian lifestyle; Akhmatova’s poetry began to take on a more prophetic stance. She saw World War One as a spiritual event, “retribution for the indifference shown by the intelligentsia and upper classes towards the suffering of the common people." She predicted horror and terror would be the future of Russia.

Of course, we wouldn’t be calling Akhmatova a prophet today if she were wrong. After Russia’s horrible failures in the war, the tsarist regime was overthrown, and the Bolsheviks came to power. Boris Anwerp, a close friend of Anna Akhmatova, recollected her initial reaction to the revolution.

“She was excited and said we must expect more changes in our lives. The same thing’s going to happen that occurred in France during the Revolution, but maybe even worse.”

It was around this time that Anna Akhmatova had to make a decision: leave Russia, like many of the intelligentsia, so that she could escape the constraints and oppression of the Bolshevik regime, or stay in her homeland. She made the decision to stay: for better or for worse. The difficulty of her decision is captured in the poem When in suicidal anguish.

During the course of the Russian Civil War, Akhmatova was moved to Sheremet’ev Palace by the Bolshevik government. Many prominent scholars and artists which they saw as possibly helpful to the revolutionary movement were housed in the palace. As the Bolsheviks were busy fighting the civil war and participated little in artistic life. This gave Akhmatova the opportunity to attend many poetry readings and write poetry. Most of the themes of these poems concern the loss of friends, specifically those who had emigrated out of Russia due to the rise of the Soviets. Akhmatova truly believed that in order for a poet to maintain their art, they must be in their native land, and so she strongly opposed emigration.

Throughout the course of the 1920s, Akhmatova would lament the fact that she had gained nothing from the revolution. Indeed, she had lost many of her close friends and the bohemian culture that she enjoyed so much prior to the war was completely gone. It was also infinitely more difficult to produce and publish the poetry she wanted to because of the ever-present Bolshevik government. Poets who had chosen not to emigrate found themselves in a tough position. They had to accommodate to the whims of the new regime in order to stay relevant, or they had to “remain consciously on the periphery” (Reeder 8). Sadly, by 1925 no one was publishing her work and she became irrelevant in Russia. For much of the 1920s-1940s, many people in the West considered her to be dead or missing because practically none of her poetry would ever be published outside of Russia, and it was barely ever published in her own country.

Life became increasingly difficult for Akhmatova under the reign of Joseph Stalin. Her first husband had been executed by the Cheka (the first Soviet secret police organization in 1921) for holding anti-communist views. Due to this, their son, Lev Gumilev, would constantly be accused of being anti-regime and forced into prison on and off for most of the 1930s up until the 1950s. This was an incredibly painful time for Anna Akhmatova. What friends she had left were routinely arrested by the secret police and sent to Siberia; she always feared that she would be the next victim, and of course, she couldn’t stop worrying for her son. Famed Lithuanian poet, Tomas Vencolva said of this time “The Stalinist terror was a sort of lottery; one might be completely loyal and might perish nevertheless”(Venclova and Hinsey 5). Since none of her work was published, hardly any money was coming in, and so she lived in poverty.

It is this experience, under the tyrannical rule of Stalin, that influenced Akhmatova to chronicle and truly capture the feeling of fear and oppression that the Russian people had under Stalin’s regime. She never quite stopped writing poetry. Indeed, between 1935 and 1961, she composed her most famous work, translated as Requiem in English. The poem was composed under incredible circumstances. Since she was terrified of the secret police finding the poem and unleashing another wave of arrests on her fellow poets, she would whisper lines from the poem to her close friend, Lydia Chukovskaya, who would memorize every single word. Akhmatova would then burn the poem in an ashtray. Chukovskaya would later recount this process as ritualistic in her now famous Akhmatova Journals, saying, “It was a ritual: hands, match, ashtray - a beautiful and mournful ritual.” The poem was first published in Russia in 1989, during perestroika.

During World War Two, Anna Akhmatova did her best to support the Russian people in their war against the Third Reich. Despite the abysmal treatment she had received from her country, Akhmatova was a patriot. She particularly advocated for the preservation of the Russian language, and was known to recite verse at military hospitals in Tashkent, where the government had sent her when the war broke out. Due to the government’s focus on the war, Akhmatova was able to publish her Izbrannoe (Selected Poems), and because her son had taken part in the war by helping capture Berlin, he was allowed to return to university.

Alas, these improvements in her life did not last for long. In 1945 and 1946 she received visits from the British scholar Isaiah Berlin. While she thoroughly enjoyed the conversations with the philosopher, they brought the attention of the government. The government criticized her work as “bourgeois” and “decadent”. She lost many of the privileges the Soviet government awarded to artists, and she once again struggled to stay alive. To make matters infinitely worse, her son was rearrested again in 1949.

Akhmatova became so desperate to get her son out of prison again that she began to write poetry that praised Stalin’s government and praised the Soviet Union. This did not work. When Stalin died in 1953, life gradually started to become easier for Akhmatova. Nikita Kruschev denounced Stalin’s reign as tyrannical and government restrictions on artistic pursuits became less severe. In the late 1950s and early 1960s, some of Akhmatova’s poetry from years prior was published, though censored by the government. Her son was released from prison for the last time in 1956.

The famed Lithuanian poet, Tomas Venclova, was acquainted with Akhmatova during this period. He said of her “In the 1960s, she was literally besieged by young people who wished to acquaint her with their scribblings and hoped for encouragement from her”(Venclova and Hinsey 5). From this, one can deduce that Akhmatova’s fame had been somewhat restored, and was a huge influence on new, aspiring poets.

She was allowed to make two trips abroad to accept a Poetry prize in Italy and to accept an honorary doctorate from Oxford. Anna Akhmatova died in 1966 of a heart attack.

It wasn’t until the late 1980s that Akhmatova became fully recognized for her poetry in her own country. By this time, the Soviet Union was disintegrating and many of her past works were being published. Akhmatova’s reputation in Russia is legendary today. No other poet had managed to capture the collective terror and numbness that all of Russia felt during Stalin’s rule quite like she had. She was the one quiet voice for millions who suffered and feared on a daily basis. Her prophetic nature and deep understanding of human suffering were unparalleled.
